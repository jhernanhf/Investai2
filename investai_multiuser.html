Module 1: Investing Basics

Understand what investing actually is — and why it matters for your future.
Beginner · ~8 min
🏦
What is investing?
Investing means putting money to work so it can grow over time. Instead of sitting in a checking account, your money buys pieces of companies, bonds, or funds — and earns returns as those assets grow in value or pay income.
💰
Saving vs. investing
Savings accounts are safe but grow slowly (often below inflation). Investing carries more risk but has historically produced much higher returns over the long run — making it essential for big goals like retirement.
📈
Why start early?
Compound growth means your gains earn gains over time. $5,000 invested at age 25 could be worth over $70,000 by retirement — just from compounding at a historical average return. Time is your most powerful tool.
⚠️
What about risk?
All investing involves risk — your money can go down in value. Short-term drops are normal. Historically, the stock market has always recovered from crashes over a long enough time horizon. Risk tolerance is personal.
✅
Golden rule before investing: Build 3–6 months of living expenses as an emergency fund first. Invest only money you won't need for at least 3–5 years.
🚫
Common mistake: Putting money you'll need soon (rent, tuition) into the stock market. Markets can drop 30–50% in a crash — only invest money you can afford to leave alone.

Module 2: Asset Types

Stocks, ETFs, bonds, index funds — what are they and how are they different?
Beginner · ~10 min
📊
Stocks (Equities)
A stock is a tiny ownership slice of a company. If Apple has 15 billion shares and you own 10, you own 10/15,000,000,000th of Apple. Stocks rise when companies grow and fall when they struggle. Higher risk, higher potential return.
🧺
ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)
An ETF is a basket of stocks or bonds you buy as one investment. VTI holds thousands of U.S. companies. Instead of picking individual stocks, you own a tiny slice of all of them at once — automatically diversified.
🏛️
Bonds
A bond is a loan you make to a company or government. They pay you interest (the coupon) and return your principal at maturity. Lower risk than stocks, lower returns too. Good for stability and ballast in a portfolio.
📉
Index Funds
Index funds track a market index like the S&P 500. Instead of a fund manager picking stocks, they simply buy everything in the index. Lower fees, less chance of human error. VOO and SPY track the S&P 500.
Popular beginner ETFs explained
🇺🇸
VTI — Total US Market
Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF. Owns ~4,000 US companies of all sizes. The broadest US exposure you can get in one ticker. Expense ratio: 0.03%.
🌍
VXUS — International
Vanguard Total International Stock ETF. Covers non-US markets including Europe, Asia, and emerging markets. Pairs well with VTI for global diversification.
📋
BND — Bonds
Vanguard Total Bond Market ETF. A mix of US government and corporate bonds. Reduces portfolio volatility — when stocks crash, bonds often stay stable or rise.
💵
SGOV — Cash-like
iShares 0-3 Month Treasury Bond ETF. Nearly as safe as a savings account but holds short-term government bonds. Good for money you'll need in 1-2 years.
💡
Beginner tip: Most financial experts agree that for most people, most of the time, a simple portfolio of a few broad index ETFs beats trying to pick individual stocks. Warren Buffett himself recommends it.

Module 3: Diversification

Don't put all your eggs in one basket — why spreading risk is the #1 rule of investing.
Beginner · ~8 min

Diversification means spreading your money across different assets so that if one investment tanks, it doesn't wipe out your whole portfolio. It's the closest thing to a free lunch in investing.

🏢
Diversify across companies
If you own only Apple stock and Apple has a bad year, your whole portfolio suffers. Owning 500+ companies means no single company can ruin you.
🗺️
Diversify across countries
The US market doesn't always lead. Adding international exposure (VXUS) means you benefit when Europe or Asia outperforms — and you're not over-exposed to a single economy.
⚖️
Diversify across asset types
Stocks and bonds often move in opposite directions. When stocks crash, bonds can hold steady. A mix of both reduces your portfolio's wild swings without giving up much long-term return.
📅
Diversify over time (DCA)
Dollar-cost averaging means investing a fixed amount every month regardless of price. You buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when high — averaging out your cost over time.
What does a diversified beginner portfolio look like?

Here's a simple three-fund portfolio many financial educators recommend for beginners:

US 60%
Intl 25%
Bonds 15%
VTI — US Stocks (60%)
VXUS — International (25%)
BND — Bonds (15%)
💡
Note: The right allocation depends on your age and risk tolerance. Younger investors can typically handle more stocks. As you near retirement, shifting more to bonds reduces volatility.
⚠️
Signs your portfolio isn't diversified: More than 10% in a single stock · All in one sector (e.g. only tech) · Only US companies · No bonds if you're over 40 · Correlated assets that all move together.

Module 4: How to Actually Invest

Where to open an account, what to buy first, and how to keep going.
Beginner · ~10 min
Step-by-step: your first investment
  • 1
    Build your emergency fund first
    Keep 3–6 months of living expenses in a high-yield savings account before investing. This prevents you from having to sell investments at a bad time when life happens.
  • 2
    Choose the right account type
    If your employer offers a 401(k) with matching, contribute enough to get the full match first — it's free money. Then open a Roth IRA (tax-free growth) or Traditional IRA. After maxing those, use a regular brokerage account.
  • 3
    Open a brokerage account
    Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab, and M1 Finance are beginner-friendly, free to open, and have no commissions. Avoid brokers that charge per trade. The account takes 1–3 business days to verify and fund.
  • 4
    Start simple — buy one ETF
    Many experts say a single broad index ETF like VTI or a target-date fund is a perfectly valid long-term strategy. Don't wait until you've researched everything. Done beats perfect.
  • 5
    Set up automatic contributions
    Set up a recurring transfer — even $25/week — on payday. Automatic investing removes emotion and builds the habit. You'll invest whether markets are up or down (dollar-cost averaging in action).
  • 6
    Rebalance once a year
    Once a year, check if your allocation has drifted from your target. If stocks grew and are now 80% of your portfolio instead of 70%, sell a little and buy bonds to rebalance. No more than once a year is usually enough.

Understanding the Risk Check metrics
📐
Beta
Measures how much your portfolio swings relative to the market. Beta of 1.0 = moves with the market. Beta of 1.5 = 50% more volatile than the market. Beta of 0.7 = less volatile than the market.
🏆
Alpha
Positive alpha means you're outperforming what your level of risk should produce. Zero or negative alpha means you could have done just as well (or better) with a simple index fund.
🌊
Volatility
How much your portfolio value bounces around day-to-day. Higher volatility = bigger swings up and down. Beginners often prefer lower volatility so they're less tempted to panic-sell.
📉
Max Drawdown
The biggest percentage drop your portfolio experienced from its peak to its lowest point. A 20% max drawdown means your portfolio once fell 20% from its high. Lower is better.

Module 6: Investing Glossary

Every term you'll encounter, explained in plain English. No jargon.
Reference · Always open
Alpha
A measure of how much better (or worse) your portfolio performs compared to what its level of risk would predict. Positive alpha = outperforming. Negative alpha = underperforming.
Asset allocation
The mix of different asset types in your portfolio — such as 70% stocks, 20% bonds, 10% cash. Your allocation should reflect your risk tolerance and time horizon.
Beta
How much your portfolio moves relative to the market. Beta of 1.0 = moves with the market. Above 1.0 = more volatile than the market. Below 1.0 = less volatile.
Bond
A loan you make to a company or government in exchange for regular interest payments and return of your principal at maturity. Generally lower risk and lower return than stocks.
Brokerage account
An account at a financial institution (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard) where you can buy and sell investments. Different from a bank account — money here is invested, not insured.
Capital gains
The profit you make when you sell an investment for more than you paid. Short-term gains (held less than 1 year) are taxed as income. Long-term gains (held 1+ year) get lower tax rates.
Compound growth
When your investment returns earn their own returns over time. $1,000 growing at 7% becomes $1,070 after year 1, then $1,144.90 after year 2 — because you earned 7% on the $1,070, not just the original $1,000.
Correlation
How closely two assets move together. A correlation of 1.0 means they always move together. A correlation of -1.0 means they move opposite. Low or negative correlation improves diversification.
Diversification
Spreading investments across many different assets, sectors, and geographies to reduce risk. If one investment falls, others may hold up — reducing the overall damage to your portfolio.
Dividend
A cash payment some companies make to shareholders, usually quarterly. Dividend-paying stocks provide regular income in addition to potential price appreciation.
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)
Investing a fixed amount at regular intervals (e.g. $200 every month) regardless of market conditions. This reduces the impact of market timing and builds investing discipline.
Drawdown
The percentage decline from a portfolio's peak value to its lowest point in a given period. A max drawdown of 30% means your portfolio once fell 30% from its high before recovering.
ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A basket of stocks or bonds that trades on an exchange like a single stock. ETFs can hold hundreds or thousands of assets, providing instant diversification. Most have very low fees.
Expense ratio
The annual fee a fund charges as a percentage of your investment. A 0.03% expense ratio on $10,000 costs $3/year. Compare: an actively managed fund might charge 1% ($100/year on the same amount).
Index fund
A fund that tracks a market index (like the S&P 500) by holding the same assets in the same proportions. No active management means lower costs and historically competitive returns.
Inflation
The gradual rise in prices over time, which erodes the purchasing power of cash. Investing aims to grow your money faster than inflation so your savings don't lose real value.
IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
A tax-advantaged account for retirement savings. Traditional IRA: tax deduction now, pay taxes later. Roth IRA: no deduction now, but all growth and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.
Market cap
The total value of a company's outstanding shares. Large-cap companies (like Apple, Microsoft) are generally more stable. Small-cap companies are riskier but can grow faster.
Portfolio
The complete collection of investments you own — stocks, ETFs, bonds, cash — across all your accounts. Your portfolio's mix is its asset allocation.
Rebalancing
Periodically adjusting your portfolio back to your target allocation. If stocks grow and become 80% of your portfolio instead of your target 70%, you'd sell some stocks and buy bonds to rebalance.
Risk tolerance
How much volatility and potential loss you can handle emotionally and financially without panic-selling. Younger investors typically have higher risk tolerance because they have time to recover from downturns.
S&P 500
An index of the 500 largest publicly traded US companies. It's often used as the benchmark for US stock market performance. ETFs like VOO and SPY track it.
Stock (Equity)
A share representing ownership in a company. Stockholders benefit when the company grows (rising share price) and may receive dividends. Stocks can also lose value if the company performs poorly.
Time horizon
How long you plan to invest before needing the money. Longer time horizons allow you to take more risk because you have more time to recover from downturns.
Volatility
How much and how quickly an investment's price changes. High volatility = large swings up and down. Low volatility = smoother ride. Measured statistically as standard deviation of returns.
